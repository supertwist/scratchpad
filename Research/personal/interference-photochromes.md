# How to Make Interference Photochromes (Lippmann Plates)

An interference photochrome — also called a Lippmann plate, after Gabriel Lippmann who won the 1908 Nobel Prize in Physics for inventing them — is a photograph that stores color as microscopic standing waves of light frozen into a special glass plate. Unlike modern color film, no dyes or pigments are used. The colors you see when you look at a finished plate are the *actual wavelengths* of the light that originally exposed it, replayed by the structure of the silver inside the gelatin. The pictures look like little glowing windows, with metallic shimmer that shifts as you tilt them.

This is real wet-chemistry photography. Plan on a full day for your first attempt and expect a few failures. The chemicals listed are toxic — read every Safety Data Sheet linked at the bottom before you begin.

---

## What you will need (overview)

- A **panchromatic ultra-fine-grain holographic plate** — the modern stand-in for hand-coated emulsion. The Russian Slavich brand "PFG-03c" is the standard. You can make your own emulsion from gelatin, silver nitrate, and potassium bromide, but the homemade version is finicky and the commercial plates are dramatically more reliable.
- A **large-format camera** that accepts the plate size you bought (most people use 4×5 inch). The lens should be fast — f/4.5 or faster helps. You expose through the glass *backwards* compared with normal film.
- A **reflective backing** to press against the back of the emulsion during exposure (this is what creates the standing waves). The original method used liquid mercury — do not use mercury. Modern substitutes are described in Step 4.
- A **dim red safelight** (the emulsion is sensitive to all visible colors, so even a regular red safelight is risky — many practitioners work in total darkness with night-vision goggles).
- **Developer chemistry** (pyrogallol, potassium bromide, and household-strength ammonia).
- **A wedged piece of glass** and **Canada balsam** for finishing the plate so you can actually see the picture.
- Standard wet-darkroom gear: trays, distilled water, nitrile gloves, splash goggles, an apron, a thermometer, a graduated cylinder, and a small kitchen scale that reads to 0.1 g.

## Step-by-step

### Step 1 — Set up a safe workspace

Work in a room with good ventilation and a sink. Lay down newspaper or a chemical-resistant mat. Have running water within reach. Wear nitrile gloves, splash-proof goggles, and a chemical-resistant apron the entire time. Keep food and drinks out of the room. Have a fan blowing fresh air across the workspace when you mix anything containing ammonia or pyrogallol — the fumes are sharp and unpleasant.

Set up your safelight (deep red, low-wattage, at least 4 feet away from where the plate will sit). Do a test by leaving an exposed-but-undeveloped plate in your safelight for two minutes, then developing it; if it darkens, your safelight is too bright. The safest workflow is to load the camera in total darkness or by feel inside a changing bag.

### Step 2 — Load the plate in the camera *backwards*

This is the key trick. In normal photography, the light-sensitive side of the film faces the lens. For a Lippmann photochrome, you flip the plate around: **the glass side faces the lens, and the emulsion side faces the back of the camera holder.** Light passes through the clear glass first, then hits the emulsion, then bounces off the mirror you're about to press against the back, then comes back through the emulsion a second time. The forward and reflected waves overlap and create the microscopic stripes that will record color.

Load the plate in a film holder in complete darkness. Wear clean cotton gloves to avoid fingerprints. Mark the holder so you know which side is the emulsion when you take it out later.

### Step 3 — Compose and expose

Set up the camera on a sturdy tripod. The exposure will be long — typically **2 to 5 minutes at f/4.5 to f/11 in bright daylight**, depending on the brightness of your scene. Bright, saturated, brightly-lit subjects work best. Stained-glass windows, a vase of flowers near a sunny window, a colored object on white paper — these are classic test subjects. The subject must not move during the entire exposure, and neither can the camera; even traffic vibration can spoil the plate. Use a cable release, lock the mirror up if your camera has one, and stand still.

Bracket your exposures wildly the first time. Make several plates of the same scene at different times (say 1 minute, 2 minutes, 4 minutes, 8 minutes). You will learn more from one bracketed set than from a dozen single guesses.

### Step 4 — Provide a mirror behind the plate

This is the historically tricky part. Lippmann pressed a small pool of liquid mercury directly against the back of the wet emulsion. **Do not do this.** Mercury vapor is seriously toxic and you cannot vent it safely at home.

Pick one of these modern alternatives:

- **The "no mirror" method (easiest, dimmer results).** Just leave a layer of air behind the emulsion. Some weak reflection happens at the gelatin-air boundary; you get a real photochrome, just less saturated. Back the plate with black velvet to absorb stray light.
- **Aluminized mylar film (recommended for beginners).** Press a fresh sheet of optically-flat aluminized mylar (the kind sold for first-surface mirrors or for solar-eclipse viewers) against the back of the plate. You want the shiny side touching the emulsion. Hold it in place gently with a piece of foam and the camera back.
- **Gallium metal (advanced).** Gallium melts at body temperature (about 86°F / 30°C) and behaves much like mercury but is not poisonous. Warm a small amount in a hot-water bath, pool it on a piece of glass, and lay the plate emulsion-down on it. Messy. Sticks to skin. Stains everything.

After exposure, remove the plate carefully in total darkness and put it in a light-tight box. Do not let it dry out completely before development — work within an hour or two.

### Step 5 — Mix the developer (do this right before you need it)

You will make two stock solutions ahead of time, then combine them at the moment of use.

**Solution A — Pyrogallol stock**
- 1 gram pyrogallol
- 100 mL isopropyl alcohol (90% or higher)

Stir gently in a sealable glass bottle until dissolved. Keep this stock in a dark bottle; it lasts a few months. **Wear a respirator while weighing the dry pyrogallol powder** — it absorbs through skin and lungs.

**Solution B — Potassium bromide stock**
- 10 grams potassium bromide
- 100 mL distilled water

Stir until dissolved. This stock lasts indefinitely.

**Working developer (mix only what you need, use once, throw away)**
- 20 mL Solution A
- 30 mL Solution B
- 140 mL distilled water
- 10 mL household ammonia (clear, no detergents, around 5–10% strength is fine; pure 25% "photographic ammonia" diluted 1:3 also works)

Add the ammonia *last*, immediately before you put the plate in. The developer is most active for about 2 minutes. Target temperature is around **60°F (15°C)** — cooler than normal film development, to slow it down enough for control.

### Step 6 — Develop the plate

Under your red safelight (or in the dark), slide the exposed plate into a flat tray and pour the working developer over it. Rock the tray gently. The image will appear faintly within 30 seconds and continue to build for 2 to 3 minutes. The plate will look murky and grayish-brown, not bright; this is normal. *You will not see colors yet.* Stop when the dark areas look medium-gray on a light box, not pitch-black.

### Step 7 — Wash, but do NOT "fix"

Pour the developer out (down the drain with lots of running water, or save for hazardous-waste pickup if your municipality requires it). Wash the plate in cold running distilled water for **at least 15 minutes**. Be gentle — the wet gelatin is fragile.

**Do not use fixer.** This is the single most important rule. In normal film photography, fixer dissolves the unexposed silver and "fixes" the image. In Lippmann photography, that step would destroy the microscopic stripe pattern that *is* the color information. The undeveloped silver halide in a Lippmann plate is so fine-grained that it stays clear and stable without fixing.

### Step 8 — Dry slowly

Stand the plate on edge, emulsion side facing nothing, in a dust-free space (a closed cardboard box with the lid cracked open works). Drying must happen evenly. **If the gelatin shrinks unevenly while it dries, the color stripes inside it shift too, and the picture changes hue or fails.** This is why your first attempts may come out the wrong color — too much shrinkage compresses the stripes and shifts everything toward blue. Let it dry overnight at room temperature.

### Step 9 — Look at the plate

In dim room light, hold the plate so that bright light is coming from *behind you* and reflecting off the emulsion at a shallow angle. You should see your image in color when viewed this way. From other angles, the plate looks dark or silvery. **It is not a transparency — you don't shine light through it. You look at the light bouncing back off it.**

If you see a recognizable image but the colors are wrong (e.g., everything blue-shifted), the gelatin shrank during drying. There are techniques to "swell" the plate back out with glycerin or triethanolamine, but for a first project just accept what you get and adjust drying for the next plate.

### Step 10 — Finish the plate (optional but worth it)

A finished Lippmann plate is mounted with a wedged piece of clear glass on top of the emulsion. This does two things: it protects the gelatin from scratches, and the wedge angle redirects unwanted surface reflections so you only see the deep, glowing color image.

To finish a plate:

1. Cut a small piece of clear glass slightly larger than the plate, and grind one edge so the glass is wedge-shaped (thicker on one side than the other) — about 10 degrees is traditional. Many practitioners just use a pre-made wedged microscope slide.
2. Place a drop of **Canada balsam** (a sticky, clear tree resin used as an optical glue since the 1800s) on the dry emulsion.
3. Lower the wedge glass onto it slowly to avoid trapping bubbles.
4. Press out excess balsam from the edges with a clean cotton swab.
5. Paint the back of the original plate (the side that faced the lens during exposure) with flat black paint. This stops stray light from washing out the image.

Let the balsam cure for several days before handling.

### The big three safety warnings, in plain language

1. **Pyrogallol is absorbed through your skin.** It is a documented toxin that can cause severe, even fatal, systemic poisoning if it soaks into your bare hands. Wear nitrile gloves (not latex — latex is permeable to it) and wear them all the time. Wear a respirator with an organic-vapor cartridge when weighing the dry powder.
2. **Silver nitrate burns and stains.** It is corrosive to skin and will permanently stain anything it touches a dark gray/black. It is also a strong oxidizer (fire risk if it contacts paper, fabric, or organic dust). It requires a DEA Form 222 to ship in the United States, which the supplier provides.
3. **Concentrated ammonia gives off fumes that can damage your lungs and eyes.** Always use it in a well-ventilated room and never lean over the open bottle.

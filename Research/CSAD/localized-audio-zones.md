# Technologies for projecting audio to a limited space in a large room

## 1. Parametric / ultrasonic directional speakers ("audio spotlight")

Emit an ultrasonic carrier modulated with the audio signal; the ultrasound only demodulates into audible sound where the beam is aimed, so sound stays confined to a narrow cone (typically 1–15 m throw) with minimal spillover into the surrounding space. This is the closest thing to "projecting" sound like a light beam and is the standard tool for exhibit-specific audio in large open galleries.

- [Holosonics — Audio Spotlight](https://www.holosonics.com/)
- [Focusonics — Parametric Speakers](https://www.focusonics.com/parametric-speakers/)
- [Audfly — What is Target Speaker? Directional Audio for Museums & Retail](https://www.audflyspeaker.com/what-is-target-speaker/)

## 2. Electronic beam-steering column arrays

Column loudspeakers with multiple independently-amplified drivers use DSP-controlled phase/delay to electronically steer one or more vertical (and in some products horizontal) sound beams toward a specific zone or audience area, without physically aiming the cabinet. Useful when you need more SPL/fidelity than a parametric speaker can deliver but still want to keep sound off of adjacent areas of a large room.

- [Bose Professional — MSA12X Powered Beam-Steering Array Loudspeaker](https://boseprofessional.com/products/loudspeakers/line-columns/powered/msa/msa12x-powered-beam-steering-array-loudspeaker)
- [Bosch/Keenfinity — LAS-BEAM Steerable Column Speakers](https://www.keenfinity-group.com/us/en/news/product-news/las-beam-steerable-column-speakers/)

## 3. Highly directional conventional transducers (horn/waveguide-loaded)

Standard-technology alternative to ultrasonic units: tightly-controlled constant-directivity horns or waveguide arrays narrow the coverage pattern of a conventional cone driver enough to isolate sound to a defined area. Lower cost and no ultrasonic-demodulation artifacts, but generally a wider/less crisp "edge" to the coverage zone than parametric speakers.

*(No single authoritative vendor link here — this is achieved with off-the-shelf constant-directivity horn speakers from most pro-audio manufacturers combined with careful aiming/architectural shielding rather than a distinct named product category.)*

## Notes for installation use

- Parametric speakers are typically overhead-mounted and aimed straight down or at a shallow angle for a "pool of sound" effect around a single exhibit — good fit for isolating a small footprint in a large room.
- Beam-steering columns are better when the target zone is larger (e.g., a seating cluster) or when you need program-quality full-range sound rather than a narrow, EQ-limited zone. *Note, Mike Kern mentions these are in the $10-12K range per unit. After looking at the product literature, it's clear this tech is used in permanent setups, like an auditorium or PA in a train station - and require a fair amoint of infrastructure.*
- Both approaches benefit from acoustically absorptive surfaces/ceilings nearby to reduce reflections re-spreading sound outside the intended zone — the electronics create directivity, but room acoustics can undo it.
